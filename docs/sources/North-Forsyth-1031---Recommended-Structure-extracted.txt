1. Executive summary
The Hanover term sheet would give Kao a 95% interest in a vehicle that also carries Hanover's promote. A promote is a profit share, which turns the vehicle into a partnership — and a partnership interest is not like-kind real property, so the exchange would fail. The recommended fix separates the two ideas in time. In Phase 1, Kao exchanges Belara into 100% fee title in North Forsyth through a qualified intermediary and a build-to-suit EAT. In Phase 2, after the exchange has genuinely seasoned, Kao contributes the property to a promoted joint venture with Hanover under §721 (tax-free). The parties keep the full term-sheet economics; the exchange remains defensible. This is a conditional, more-likely-than-not position whose strength rises with seasoning — not a “clean” or automatic result.
2. The two phases
Phase 1 — the exchange (Days 0–180). Belara proceeds park with the QI. The EAT takes title to North Forsyth, borrows the construction loan, and builds. By Day 180 the EAT deeds 100% fee title to Kao's disregarded single-member LLC. The exchange completes against direct real estate, so there is no partnership to recharacterize on the entity-classification axis.
Phase 2 — the JV (after genuine seasoning). Kao contributes the property to a new operating partnership under §721; Hanover converts its Phase-1 loan into a 5% interest and takes the promote. The JV carries the full term sheet: 95/5, the 20/30/40 promote over 10/14/18, the 4% and 4.5% fees, and the guaranties.
3. Why this survives where the term sheet fails
100% real property in Phase 1. A single owner cannot be a partnership; Rev. Proc. 2002-22 never comes into play. A partnership interest is not real property under §1031(a)(1) and Reg. §1.1031(a)-3 — so Kao takes real property, not an interest in a recharacterizable co-ownership.
Promote lives in the Phase-2 partnership. A promote is perfectly legal inside a partnership and keeps capital-gain character as a profits interest (§1061 then requires a >3-year hold for long-term rates).
The residual risk is step-transaction, and it is managed, not ignored. See seasoning and neutrality, below.

6. Neutrality & Hanover protection (Phase 1 stands on its own)
Hanover is protected as a contractor and creditor through standalone, arm's-length contracts — not by an unenforceable promise of future equity. All five elements go together:
Standalone market Phase-1 contracts — a development management agreement at a market 4% fee and a GMP/GC contract at a market 4.5%, each with normal reimbursement, lien rights, and default remedies.
Hanover's 5% as a true secured LOAN bearing interest — ~$1.1M funded as debt (note + collateral, market rate, stated maturity), not a “preferred return.” It converts to a 5% interest via §721 only in Phase 2.
Kao guaranty-indemnity backed by collateral/LC — a real, fundable obligation covering Hanover's Phase-1 exposure.
Fixed or cost-based termination fee if Kao walks — documented costs, fees earned, and a reasonable margin; it must not mirror the promote/IRR waterfall.
LOI expressly non-binding; no fixed-price equity option — protection comes from the commercial contracts and the secured loan, not a pre-signed partnership.

8. Structure B — the stronger fallback
Kao owns 100% permanently and Hanover is a fee developer whose promote is replicated as an ordinary-income incentive fee (same hurdles, measured at sale/refi/appraisal). With a single owner there is no partnership question, so this is the strongest version of the exchange. The trade-off is that Hanover's incentive fee is ordinary income rather than capital gain — gross it up to keep Hanover whole. Use Structure B when the parties want the strongest, simplest exchange; use Structure A when Hanover requires true ownership and capital-gain carry.
