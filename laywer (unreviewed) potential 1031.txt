Selling an apartment complex for $20 million and completing a §1031 like-kind exchange into a to-be-built industrial building is very doable, but it requires precise structuring, timing, and professional coordination..and remember..ownership has to stay be in the same entity.Below is the clean, real-world way this is typically done.



⸻



1. Confirm the Transaction Qualifies for a 1031 Exchange



A 1031 exchange (under Section 1031 of the Internal Revenue Code) allows you to defer capital gains taxes if:



Both properties are held for investment or business use

The replacement property is like-kind (industrial qualifies for multifamily)

You follow strict timelines

You never take constructive receipt of the sale proceeds



Oversight comes from the Internal Revenue Service, so documentation and compliance matter.



⸻



2. Sell the Apartment Complex (Relinquished Property)



Key steps:



Put the apartment complex under contract

Before closing, engage a Qualified Intermediary (QI)

    This is mandatory



At closing:



Sale proceeds go directly to the QI

You do not touch the money

Let’s assume net exchange proceeds ≈ $20M



⸻



3. Identify the Replacement Property (Within 45 Days)



You have 45 days from the sale closing to formally identify the replacement property in writing.



For a to-be-built industrial project, you typically identify:



The land

The construction project

Often via a reverse or improvement exchange structure



Identification rules commonly used:



3-Property Rule, or

200% Rule



You must clearly describe:



Address / parcel

Intended improvements

Estimated total value (must meet or exceed $20M)



⸻



4. Use a Construction (Improvement) 1031 Exchange Structure



Since the industrial building does not yet exist, you’ll use a 1031 Improvement Exchange (sometimes called a Build-to-Suit Exchange).



How this works structurally:



The QI forms or uses an Exchange Accommodation Titleholder (EAT)

The EAT temporarily holds title to the land

Exchange funds are used to:

Buy the land

Pay construction costs

Improvements must be completed within 180 days



⚠️ Only improvements completed within the 180-day exchange window count toward exchange value



⸻



5. Meet the 180-Day Exchange Deadline



You must complete all of the following within 180 days of selling the apartments:



Acquire the replacement property (via EAT)

Complete as much construction as possible

Ensure total value ≥ $20M (purchase + improvements)



If only $16M is completed by Day 180:



You’ll have $4M of taxable “boot”



⸻



6. Financing the Industrial Project (Very Common)



If the industrial project costs more than $20M:



You can add:

Bank construction loans

Mezzanine debt

Partner equity

Debt does not break a 1031 exchange

You must replace equal or greater debt than was paid off on the apartments



⸻



7. Title Transfers at the End



Once the 180 days expire:



The EAT transfers title of the improved industrial property to you

The exchange is complete

Capital gains tax is deferred, not forgiven



⸻



8. Professional Team You Must Have



This transaction cannot be DIY. You need:



Qualified Intermediary (with improvement exchange experience)

Real estate attorney (1031-savvy)

CPA / tax advisor

Construction lender

Industrial developer or GC

Title & escrow professionals familiar with EATs



⸻



9. Common Pitfalls to Avoid



❌ Missing the 45-day identification deadline

❌ Under-building by Day 180

❌ Taking possession of sale proceeds

❌ Poorly drafted identification language

❌ Using a QI without construction exchange experience



⸻



10. Practical Reality Check



For a $20M exchange into a to-be-built industrial asset, many investors:



Exchange into land + partially built structure

Finish remaining construction after the exchange window using non-exchange funds



This preserves tax deferral while allowing full project completion.



⸻



Bottom Line



Yes — you can absolutely sell a $20M apartment complex and 1031 into a to-be-built industrial building, but only through a properly structured improvement exchange with an EAT and strict adherence to timelines.
