North Forsyth Commerce Center · §1031 Exchange

Structure A — Exchange First, Then Combine

Kao exchanges Belara into 100% direct ownership of North Forsyth, then — after a genuine seasoning period — forms the promoted Hanover joint venture by a tax-free §721 contribution. Same economics as the term sheet, and the exchange is defensible — a more-likely-than-not position that strengthens toward "should" as seasoning lengthens (not "clean," not automatic).

Why this works where the term sheet failed. A promote cannot live inside the vehicle Kao receives in the exchange — a profit share turns a co-ownership into a partnership, and a partnership interest is not "real property" (§1031 is real-property-only post-TCJA; Reg. §1.1031(a)-3). Structure A keeps the promote out of the exchange: Kao receives 100% real property, then — after the exchange has seasoned — the parties form the promoted JV, in a partnership where a promote is perfectly legal and keeps capital-gain treatment.
How strong is this, honestly? More likely than not (MLTN) — not "clean" and not automatic. The §721 step has support (Magneson) but the case law has real limits, and the IRS can attack the two phases under the step-transaction doctrine. The position strengthens toward "should" as seasoning lengthens and an independent business purpose is documented. If the parties want the strongest possible exchange, Structure B (Kao 100% forever + an ordinary-income incentive fee) is the safer trade. This material keeps A primary as an informed choice, but presents both honestly. No position in this document rises to "will."

Phase 1 — The Exchange (Days 0–180)

Belara proceeds park with the Qualified Intermediary. An Exchange Accommodation Titleholder takes title to North Forsyth, borrows the construction loan, and builds. By Day 180 it deeds 100% fee title to Kao's single-member LLC. The exchange completes against direct real estate, so there is no partnership to recharacterize on the entity-classification axis.

Phase 2 — The JV (after genuine seasoning)

After a genuine seasoning gap, Kao contributes the property to a new operating partnership ("PropCo JV") under §721 — tax-free. Hanover converts its loan into a 5% interest and takes the promote. The JV carries the full term sheet: 95/5, the 20/30/40 promote, the 4% and 4.5% fees, and the guaranties.

What changed from the original GitHub diagrams. No development-TIC, no partition waiver, and no "promote-as-fee." Those were the three high-risk positions. Here the replacement is taken as 100% fee ownership, and the promote is delivered through a partnership formed after a seasoned exchange.

How it works, and why it survives §1031

The core trade-off

You cannot put a true tenancy-in-common and an enforceable promote in the same vehicle at the same time. A clean TIC (what §1031 needs) requires pro-rata economics and bars any fee tied to income or profits (Rev. Proc. 2002-22 §§6.08, 6.11, 6.12, 6.15). A promote is, by definition, a profit share. Force both into one vehicle and the IRS recharacterizes it as a partnership — meaning Kao received a partnership interest, not real property, and the exchange fails. The fix is to separate them in time: real-property acquisition first, promoted partnership second.

Why Phase 1 is strong on entity classification

Kao receives 100% fee title, so on the entity-classification axis there is nothing to attack: a single owner cannot be a partnership, and Rev. Proc. 2002-22 never comes into play. This is necessary but not sufficient — see step-transaction. A partnership interest is not "real property" for §1031 (§1031(a)(1) limits like-kind exchanges to real property; Reg. §1.1031(a)-3 confirms a partnership interest does not qualify), so taking 100% real property is the whole point.

Why a later §721 contribution can be respected — and the limits of the case law

The leading authorities are Magneson (753 F.2d 1490 (9th Cir. 1985)) and Bolker (760 F.2d 1039 (9th Cir. 1985)). They support the idea that continuing an investment (rather than cashing out) can satisfy §1031's "held for" requirement notwithstanding a planned subsequent transaction. Read them honestly — do not overstate them:

  • Old and circuit-limited. Both are pre-1984, Ninth Circuit decisions. Georgia is in the Eleventh Circuit, not bound by them; there is no on-point 11th Cir. or Supreme Court holding blessing this exact fact pattern.
  • Bolker is not a §721 case. It involved a liquidating distribution to a sole shareholder who then exchanged — it should not be cited as direct §721 authority.
  • Magneson involved an immediate, modest general-partnership rollover, not a leveraged ground-up EAT build dropped into a promoted LLC. The economics and timing here are materially more aggressive.
  • The IRS does not concede the point and can deploy the step-transaction doctrine to treat Kao as having acquired a partnership interest from the outset — which is not like-kind.
The real residual risk: step-transaction. The genuine exposure is whether the IRS collapses Phase 1 and Phase 2 into a single integrated acquisition of a partnership interest (the binding-commitment, mutual-interdependence, and end-result tests). Structure A manages (does not eliminate) this through: no binding contribution obligation at the exchange; a genuine seasoning gap measured from the deed date; Kao operating as 100% owner in the interim; a documented independent business purpose; and avoiding a §707 disguised sale. With these, the position is realistically MLTN, improving toward "should" as seasoning lengthens.

To defer the full ~$20M of Belara gain, Kao must reinvest ~$20M in completed real property by Day 180:

For a ~$50.3M ground-up project, getting $13.5M of verifiable, in-place improvements on top of the land within 180 days is the central feasibility risk. Any shortfall is boot, taxed in 2026.

Three clocks & a real cushion.
  • Day 45: identify North Forsyth in writing and a backup replacement — a specific, named, 1031-eligible DST offering, closable within 180 days if the build lags.
  • Day 180: the EAT must convey by Day 180 or the extended return due date, whichever is earlier — so extend the 2026 return.
  • Valuation: commission a Day-180 in-place valuation so the amount actually reinvested is documented (see Money flow for the boot schedule).

Equity exhaustion — what funds the rest of the build

Kao's ~$20M is fully deployed into land + the first ~$13.5M of improvements before Day 180. Everything after that — the remaining ~$30M of the $50.3M project — is funded by the construction loan (~$29M) and Hanover's ~$1.1M, both as DEBT with guaranties, never as Hanover equity. Treating any of that funding as Hanover equity in Phase 1 would create the partnership the exchange cannot have. Keep the Phase-1 stack strictly: Kao equity (exchanged) + third-party construction loan + Hanover secured loan.

Who does what in Phase 1

  • Kao sells through the QI and never controls the cash. Forms Kao NFCC Owner LLC (disregarded SMLLC; sole member = the exact Belara seller) to receive 100% title.
  • The EAT (NFCC Parking Title LLC) is the legal owner/borrower/builder during parking under Rev. Proc. 2000-37. It must be independent (not a disqualified person), bankruptcy-remote, party to a written QEAA, with no agency language making it Kao's agent.
  • Hanover builds under a standalone FMV Development Management Agreement (4%) and a GMP/GC contract (4.5%), and gives the loan guaranties. Its 5% goes in now as a secured, interest-bearing loan (note + collateral), converting to equity only in Phase 2. Hanover is not on title during the exchange.

Phase-1 mechanics to nail down (with counsel)

  • QI/EAT independence: confirm neither is a disqualified person (Reg. §1.1031(k)-1(k)).
  • QEAA + timeline (Rev. Proc. 2000-37): written QEAA within 5 business days of the EAT taking title; EAT holds qualified indicia of ownership; identify the relinquished property within 45 days of parking; combined parking period ≤ 180 days.
  • Lender consent (twice): to lend to the EAT, and to the later transfer to Kao and the Phase-2 JV — addressing due-on-sale.
  • Title: EAT-to-Kao deed chain, owner's policy and endorsements, recording — Georgia counsel.
  • Guaranty routing & EAT bankruptcy-remoteness documented so the at-risk party is protected without creating equity.

The guardrails that make Phase 2 safe. Seasoning — the clock that makes or breaks Structure A

Seasoning is the single most important variable — the real time, real ownership, and real risk-bearing between the exchange and the §721 contribution that defeats a step-transaction argument. There is no statutory safe-harbor period; longer and more genuine is always safer.

When the clock starts (commonly gotten wrong). The seasoning clock runs from the date the EAT deeds the property to Kao (completion of the exchange), not from Day 0. The 180-day parking period is part of the exchange, not part of seasoning. Counting from Day 0 to claim "six months seasoned at Day 180" is exactly the pre-wiring the IRS looks for.

What "operating as owner" must look like during the gap

  • Kao (through its disregarded SMLLC) holds 100% fee title and is named on title, the loan, leases, and insurance.
  • Kao bears the economic benefits and burdens: funds shortfalls (via debt, not Hanover equity), receives net cash flow, takes depreciation.
  • No binding obligation to contribute — any forward understanding is a non-binding LOI with a non-binding JV term sheet attached; never a signed contribution agreement, never a fixed-price equity option for Hanover.
  • An independent business purpose for combining later is documented in real time (completion/lease-up de-risking, refinancing, bringing in the operating partner once stabilized).
Bottom line: the longer the gap and the more genuinely Kao behaves as the 100% owner, the closer the opinion moves to "should." A short or mechanical gap pushes it back toward "fails." Do not let construction or financing pressure compress the gap.

Neutrality & Hanover protection — making Phase 1 stand on its own

The central critique: it is not neutral to let Hanover do all the building and funding in Phase 1 on a handshake that it will get ownership and the promote in Phase 2. If Hanover's Phase-1 role looks like disguised equity or a pre-wired partnership, the step-transaction argument strengthens. The fix is to make every Phase-1 relationship a standalone, arm's-length, market commercial contract that would make sense even if Phase 2 never happened — and to protect Hanover with real creditor and contractual remedies instead of an unenforceable promise.

  1. Standalone market Phase-1 contracts. Development role = a DMA at a market 4% fee; construction role = a GMP/GC contract at a market 4.5%; both with normal reimbursement, lien rights, and default remedies, priced as if to a third party — not as consideration for future equity.
  2. Hanover's 5% goes in as a true secured LOAN bearing interest — not a "preferred return." ~$1.1M funded as debt (Hanover NFCC Capital LLC), evidenced by a promissory note at a market rate, secured, with stated maturity and default remedies. "Preferred return" is equity language and undercuts debt characterization — don't use it. The loan converts to a 5% interest via §721 in Phase 2.
  3. Kao guaranty-indemnity, backed by collateral/LC. Because Hanover provides guaranties and does at-risk work before it owns anything, Kao gives Hanover a guaranty/indemnity for Hanover's Phase-1 exposure, collateralized (pledged collateral or a letter of credit) so it is a real, fundable obligation.
  4. Fixed / cost-based termination fee if Kao walks — not promote-mirroring. If Kao declines Phase 2, Hanover is made whole through a fixed or cost-based break fee (documented costs, fees earned, reasonable margin). It must not replicate the promote/IRR waterfall — a promote-mirroring walk-away payment would itself look like disguised equity.
  5. LOI expressly non-binding; no fixed-price equity option. Any Phase-2 understanding is a non-binding LOI (with a non-binding JV term sheet). Hanover gets no enforceable right to acquire equity at a fixed price; Kao has no binding obligation to contribute. Protection comes from the commercial contracts and the secured loan — not a pre-signed partnership.
Why this matters for the opinion: done this way, Phase 1 is economically coherent on its own (market fees + a secured, interest-bearing loan + collateralized indemnity), Hanover is genuinely protected as a contractor and creditor, and there is no binding commitment that lets the IRS argue the partnership existed from Day 0.

Tax mechanics — the partnership questions the exchange overview skips

The §1031 analysis is necessary but not the whole story. The Phase-2 §721 contribution and the debt structure raise four partnership-tax issues that must be modeled before relying on this structure. Each can create current gain even if the exchange itself succeeds.

§707 — disguised sale (the timing trap)

  • 2-year presumption (Reg. §1.707-3): transfers between a partner and a partnership within two years of each other are presumed a sale; outside two years, presumed not. Rebuttable, but sets the baseline.
  • Live issue: how Hanover's Phase-1 loan → Phase-2 equity conversion is treated, and whether Kao's funding/distribution pattern looks like a sale.
  • Not just a label: "the loan converts via §721" hides real mechanics — whether Hanover contributes the note or cash, how the JV's assumption/cancellation of the debt is treated, any §108 COD consequences, and resulting basis. Analyze it; don't paper it as one line.
  • Plan: sequence the conversion and any distributions; document non-sale intent; keep the seasoning gap long.

§752 — liability shifts (this one can surprise Kao)

  • Hanover guaranties can make the ~$29M debt recourse to the guarantor and shift liability share away from Kao (Reg. §1.752-2). A decrease in Kao's liability share is a deemed cash distribution under §752(b).
  • Consequence: that deemed distribution can exceed Kao's basis and trigger current gain — potentially undoing part of the deferral.
  • Plan: model Kao's basis and liability share before finalizing guaranties; consider bottom-dollar/vertical-slice limits or a Kao guaranty of a sufficient slice.

§704(c) — built-in gain

  • The property enters the JV with built-in gain (FMV over Kao's carryover basis); §704(c) allocates that gain back to Kao on later sale/depreciation.
  • Recommended method: "remedial" — cleanest for keeping the deferred Belara gain with Kao and giving Hanover correct depreciation; confirm with the CPA.

Basis & depreciation (the long tail)

  • Carryover basis: Kao's low Belara basis carries into North Forsyth (§1031(d)); deferral, not erasure.
  • Unrecaptured §1250 gain rides along and is taxed up to 25% when gain is recognized.
  • §263A UNICAP applies to the construction — interest/carrying costs largely capitalized.
  • Later-sale waterfall: (1) §704(c) built-in Belara gain to Kao; (2) unrecaptured §1250 at 25%; (3) remaining gain 95/5 with the promote above hurdles; (4) Georgia tax on GA-source gain — or roll again under §1031.
None of these are reasons not to do Structure A — they are reasons to model §752 and §707 with the CPA before signing, because either can create cash tax in a year with no liquidity event.

References

Statute & regulations

  • IRC §1031 — like-kind exchanges; real-property only post-TCJA (§1031(a)(1)), so partnership interests can't qualify. Current §1031(a)(2) is the "held primarily for sale" exception; the old partnership-interest exclusion at "(a)(2)(D)" was removed by the 2017 TCJA (cite (a)(1) + Reg. §1.1031(a)-3, not the dead (a)(2)(D)).
  • Treas. Reg. §1.1031(a)-3 — definition of "real property"; confirms a partnership interest is not real property.
  • IRC §721 — nonrecognition on contribution to a partnership.
  • IRC §704(c) — built-in gain allocation (recommend remedial method).
  • IRC §707(a)(2)(B) — disguised-sale rule.
  • Treas. Reg. §1.707-3 — disguised-sale mechanics; the 2-year presumption.
  • IRC §752 — partnership liabilities; a decrease in a partner's share is a deemed distribution.
  • Treas. Reg. §1.752-2 — recourse liability allocation; how guaranties shift debt.
  • IRC §1061 — carried-interest 3-year holding period (limits, does not create, capital gain).
  • Treas. Reg. §1.1031(k)-1 — QI / constructive-receipt safe harbor; disqualified-person rules at (k).
  • Treas. Reg. §301.7701-3 — disregarded entity.

Revenue procedures & case law

  • Rev. Proc. 2000-37 — QEAA / build-to-suit parking (5-business-day QEAA; qualified indicia of ownership; ≤180-day parking).
  • Rev. Proc. 2004-51 — no safe harbor for taxpayer-pre-owned property.
  • Rev. Proc. 2002-22 — TIC co-ownership ruling guidelines (advance-ruling guidance, not a safe harbor).
  • Read the case law with its limits: both are pre-1984, 9th Cir.; Georgia is in the 11th Cir.
  • Magneson v. Commissioner, 753 F.2d 1490 (9th Cir. 1985) — post-exchange §721 contribution respected, but an immediate, modest general-partnership rollover, not a leveraged EAT build into a promoted LLC.
  • Bolker v. Commissioner, 760 F.2d 1039 (9th Cir. 1985) — supports "held for investment" despite a planned later transaction; caveat: not a §721 case (liquidating distribution).
